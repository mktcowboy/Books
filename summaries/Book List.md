## Priority Reading Order

### Phase 1 — Fill the Foundations

*These are books your entire existing shelf references or builds on. Read these first.*

| # | Book | Author | Why It's Foundational |
|---|------|--------|-----------------------|
| 1 | Thinking, Fast and Slow | Daniel Kahneman | The single biggest gap — System 1/System 2 underpins half your shelf. You have his *Noise* but not his masterwork. |
| 2 | The Art of War | Sun Tzu | The most-referenced strategy text in history. The primary source your Greene and Freedman books build on. |
| 3 | The Prince | Niccolo Machiavelli | The original text on political realism. Greene and Pfeffer explicitly build on this — you have the derivatives but not the source. |
| 4 | Poor Charlie's Almanack | Charlie Munger | The wisdom capstone — Munger's latticework of mental models drawn from psychology, physics, biology, economics. |
| 5 | Letters from a Stoic | Seneca | You have Marcus Aurelius and Epictetus but not Seneca — the most practical and rhetorically skilled of the three great Stoics. |
| 6 | The Black Swan | Nassim Nicholas Taleb | You have *Antifragile* (the response) but not the original problem statement about rare, unpredictable events. |
| 7 | Getting to Yes | Roger Fisher & William Ury | The foundational negotiation text. Voss positions himself against this — reading both gives the complete picture. |
| 8 | High Output Management | Andy Grove | Intel's legendary CEO on management as a production system. The most influential management book in Silicon Valley. |
| 9 | The Art of Seduction | Robert Greene | The missing Greene book — his taxonomy of seductive character types and influence through attraction rather than force. |
| 10 | Good Strategy Bad Strategy | Richard Rumelt | The best modern book on what strategy actually IS. His "kernel" framework cuts through the fluff. |
