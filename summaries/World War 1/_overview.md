# World War 1

> Ten books that cover the Great War from every angle — why it started, how it was fought, what it felt like, and why the peace that followed was worse than the war itself. Two books on origins (with competing interpretations), three on the fighting, two on the human and cultural cost, and one on the aftermath that shaped the entire 20th century.

---

## Why This Section Exists

World War 1 is the hinge of modern history. Before it, Europe was confident, imperial, and hierarchical. After it, the old certainties were shattered — and the peace settlement created the conditions for everything that followed: Nazism, WW2, the Cold War, the Middle East. Your wife cannot understand the 20th century without understanding this war.

## Suggested Reading Order

**Phase 1 — Why it happened (read these first):**
1. **Clark (The Sleepwalkers)** — The revisionist case: Europe stumbled into war through shared miscalculation, not German aggression
2. **MacMillan (The War That Ended Peace)** — The structural case: decades of alliance-building, arms races, and imperial rivalry made war almost inevitable

*Reading both gives her two lenses. Neither is entirely right. The truth is in the overlap.*

**Phase 2 — The war itself:**
3. **Tuchman (The Guns of August)** — Start with August 1914. The mobilisations, the Schlieffen Plan, the first month's chaos. Sets the scene brilliantly
4. **Keegan (The First World War)** — The definitive military overview. Read this as the backbone of the war years
5. **Meyer (A World Undone)** — Same scope as Keegan but written for general readers. More narrative, more accessible. They complement rather than repeat each other
6. **Horne (The Price of Glory)** — Verdun. The battle that symbolises the war's futility — 700,000 casualties in 10 months for a few miles of mud
7. **Hart (The Great War)** — The soldier's war. Built on firsthand testimonies. What it actually felt like to be there

**Phase 3 — What it cost:**
8. **Fussell (The Great War and Modern Memory)** — How the war destroyed Western culture's confidence and created literary modernism. The most intellectually ambitious book in the list
9. **Brittain (Testament of Youth)** — The war from a woman's perspective. She lost her fiance, her brother, and her two closest friends. Devastating

**Phase 4 — What came next:**
10. **MacMillan (Paris 1919)** — The peace conference that created the 20th century's problems. The Treaty of Versailles, the carving-up of the Middle East, the seeds of WW2

## The Big Debates These Books Address

### Why did the war start?
- **Clark's answer:** No single villain. All the great powers sleepwalked into catastrophe through miscalculation, alliance obligations, and mobilisation timetables that couldn't be stopped
- **MacMillan's answer:** Structural forces — decades of arms races, colonial rivalries, alliance systems — made war likely. Specific decisions in July 1914 made it inevitable
- **The traditional view (challenged by Clark):** Germany bears primary responsibility through the Schlieffen Plan and aggressive diplomacy

### Was the Western Front strategy a failure of generalship?
- **The "lions led by donkeys" myth:** British soldiers were brave; their generals were incompetent butchers
- **The revisionist view (Hart, Keegan):** The generals faced impossible problems — industrial warfare with pre-industrial tactics. Attrition was not stupidity; it was the only available strategy given the technology
- **The truth:** Somewhere between. Some generals learned; others didn't. The technology favoured defence until 1918

### Was the peace worse than the war?
- **MacMillan (Paris 1919):** The peacemakers tried their best but created impossible contradictions — self-determination for some nations but not others, punitive reparations that humiliated Germany, borders that pleased no one
- **Keynes (not in the list but referenced by MacMillan):** The economic terms of Versailles guaranteed another war
- **The connection to WW2:** Every book on WW2 origins traces back to decisions made at Versailles

## Key Themes Across the Books

### The End of the Old Order
- Four empires fell: Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German
- The aristocratic officer class that started the war was destroyed by it
- Democratic, nationalist, and revolutionary movements filled the vacuum

### Industrial War
- Technology outran tactics — machine guns, artillery, gas, barbed wire made attack suicidal
- The war was won by logistics and production, not cavalry charges
- This is why Overy's *Why The Allies Won* (in the WW2 section) is the natural sequel

### The Cultural Break
- Fussell argues the war created modern irony — the gap between what leaders said and what soldiers experienced
- Before WW1: heroism, duty, sacrifice were taken at face value. After: they were seen as lies
- This cultural shift is essential background for understanding the interwar period and WW2
