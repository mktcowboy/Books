# Geo-Strategy — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

> A 12-lecture series on contemporary geopolitics — focusing on the Middle East conflict, American empire, Russian strategy, and the emerging multipolar world. Prof. Jiang applies historical patterns from his Civilization series to analyse current events, with particular focus on asymmetrical warfare, imperial hubris, and the decline of American hegemony.

---

## About the Lecturer

Prof. Jiang Xueqin brings a historian's perspective to contemporary geopolitics. He analyses current events through the same frameworks he uses for ancient civilizations — empire cycles, hubris, asymmetric strategy, and the tension between military dominance and strategic flexibility. His Geo-Strategy lectures are explicitly connected to his Civilization series, applying its historical lessons to the present.

## Recurring Themes

- **Religion as civilisation driver** — religion is not a private belief but a structural force shaping empires, wars, and foreign policy (Lectures 1-2); deism and the Declaration of Independence shaped America's wealth-creation mission (Lecture 3); the Sunni-Shia split as a 7th-century succession dispute driving 21st-century proxy wars (Lecture 4)
- **Empire cycles** — Pax Romana / Pax Americana pattern: imperial peace produces inequality, inequality produces radical movements, radical movements produce war (Lecture 2); financialisation as the mechanism through which empire creates vulnerability and makes war inevitable (Lecture 3); the two theories of empire as the generational cycle: builders (1991 restraint) vs. inheritors (2003 hubris) — the billionaire father analogy (Lecture 6)
- **Imperial hubris** — the US's inability to recognise that its Middle East policy is driven by theology rather than strategy (Lectures 1-2); expanded to a two-mechanism structural model: imposed reality + preference for arrogance (Lecture 3); Trump choosing "Option 3" — the extreme option designed to be unchosen — as a manifestation of hubris (Lecture 4); now the definitive treatment: institutional capture by one anomalous victory (Iraq 2003), generational inexperience (war as video game), and the Rumsfeld effect (civilian override of military expertise) combine to guarantee the military agrees to a war it cannot win (Lecture 6)
- **The role of inequality in driving conflict** — peace produces inequality, inequality produces desperation, desperation produces extremism (Lecture 2); financialisation produces rentier economy, elite capture, and permanent wealth gaps (Lecture 3)
- **Organised minorities defeating passive majorities** — dispensationalists as a model for how fanatical minorities drive policy (Lecture 2); Wahhabis (20-40% of Saudi population) forcing the Saudi government to export extremism rather than confront it domestically (Lecture 4); the IRGC (revolutionary fanatics) controlling policy against the wishes of the Iranian population, which wants economic development and peace (Lecture 7)
- **Addiction to easy money** — financialised economy creates speculative mindset that makes reindustrialisation culturally impossible; the empire cannot survive without easy money but easy money is destroying it (Lecture 3)
- **Communism as check on capitalism** — fear of communist ideology (not the Soviet military) forced America to treat workers well 1950-1980; without an opposing ideology, no internal check on imperial behaviour (Lecture 3)
- **Asymmetrical warfare defeats conventional superiority** — the Millennium Challenge prediction (Lecture 1) confirmed by Saudi Arabia's defeat in Yemen: $10,000 drones destroying billion-dollar infrastructure, 150,000 troops losing to mountain villagers (Lecture 4); Vietnamese tunnel warfare as the historical precedent — peasants who could "swim underground" using abandoned US ammunition, unexploded bombs, and weapons sold by corrupt allies (Lecture 6); the Basij versus Iraqi tanks in the Iran-Iraq War as the original proof of concept — teenagers with rifles and keys to heaven exhausted a conventionally superior army (Lecture 7)
- **Empire-democracy tension** — the military concluded after Vietnam that democracy is the enemy of empire; shock and awe was designed to divorce imperial warfare from democratic accountability (no draft, no media access, no Congressional approval); the Pentagon Papers as the crystallising moment when the military decided civilian oversight was the problem (Lecture 6)
- **Foreign policy as purchasable commodity** — the MBS-Kushner-Trump triangle demonstrates how a desperate state can buy direct access to presidential decision-making, with $2 billion as the financial evidence (Lecture 4); Haley's $10M post-government wealth from anti-Iran donors (United Against a Nuclear Iran, Boeing, Christians United for Israel) as the mechanism for positioning a VP who will agitate for war (Lecture 5)
- **Politics as theatre** — political feuds are staged performances for voter consumption, not genuine conflicts; the Trump-Haley feud follows the same logic as WWE entertainment; the redemption narrative (picking a former rival as VP) is a replicable template, not spontaneous magnanimity (Lecture 5)
- **Analytical model-building** — build testable predictions from structural data, observe reality, refine the model when wrong; the purpose of prediction is not to be right but to improve the model over time (Lecture 5); most thorough application in Lecture 7: hypothesis (IRGC killed Raisi) → predictions (Mokhber wins, rhetoric escalates, proxies intensify, nuclear programme accelerates) → test against observed reality
- **1979 as the earthquake** — the Iranian Revolution keeps generating new consequences 45 years later; Lecture 4 covered external effects (Saudi-Iran rivalry, Sunni-Shia conflict); Lecture 7 covers internal effects (IRGC founding, embassy hostage crisis, revolutionary DNA that persists into the present military leadership)
- **Religion as civilisational driver (continued)** — the Basij's willingness to die with keys to heaven demonstrates religion as a military force multiplier, not merely a cultural identity (Lecture 7); connects to dispensationalist premillennialism driving US policy (Lecture 2) and the Sunni-Shia split driving proxy wars (Lecture 4); Christian nationalism as the most likely victor in the Second American Civil War because religious warriors are the most willing to fight and die (Lecture 11)
- **The trap pattern across history** — superior powers walk into traps set by inferior opponents because hubris prevents them from seeing what's happening: Athens in Sicily (415 BCE), America in Vietnam, Russia in Ukraine, and now America in Iran (Lecture 8); the pattern has repeated for 2,400 years because the psychology of empire never changes
- **Warrior culture vs. consumer culture** — Putin's central insight that consumer societies cannot fight wars because consumerism produces cowardice, alienation, and debt; warrior cultures (Russia, Germany, Japan) consistently defeat consumer cultures until nuclear weapons intervene (Lecture 9); explains why European NATO allies are functionally useless and why America's military dominance depends on technology, not will (Lectures 9-10)
- **Russian strategic imagination** — a philosophical tradition fundamentally different from Western empiricism: broad, mystical, intuitive rather than narrow, logical, bureaucratic; explains both Stalin's WWII masterstroke and Putin's five-front strategy against the American empire (Lecture 10); British philosophy "bureaucratised the imagination" — the West can build better weapons but cannot imagine the future (Lecture 10)
- **America's structural fragmentation** — not a political crisis but a civilisational one: two irreconcilable founding visions (Pilgrims vs. Founding Fathers), the death of every unifying narrative, collapse of every trusted institution, over-militarisation at every level of society, and a rentier economy that makes the top 1% richer while everyone else gets poorer (Lecture 11); connects to financialisation (Lecture 3), electoral dynamics (Lecture 5), and imperial hubris (Lecture 6)
- **Historical patterns as predictive science** — the series finale argues that AI could make Asimov's psychohistory real by modelling civilisational trajectories through supervised machine learning; however, the Great Man (Homer, Dante, Putin) is the edge case that breaks all prediction — you cannot model someone who intentionally defies the system (Lecture 12); the human heart (love, meaning, purpose) is the fundamental variable that determines whether civilisations flourish or collapse (Lecture 12)
