# British History

> The full sweep of the English and British story — from the first settlers after the Ice Age to post-war modern Britain. Fifteen books that cover every major era: Anglo-Saxon, Norman, Plantagenet, Tudor, Stuart, Georgian, Imperial, Victorian, and modern. Three multi-volume series (Schama, Ackroyd, and the period specialists) give overlapping coverage from different angles, while Ferguson and Olusoga provide the empire debate from both sides.

---

## Why This Section Exists

Your wife wants to understand Britain — not a textbook version, but the living story. How a small, rainy archipelago became the world's dominant power, what it did with that power, and what happened when it lost it. These fifteen books, read together, give her the complete picture — and enough conflicting interpretations to form her own judgement.

## Suggested Reading Order

**Start here — the backbone:**
1. **Robert Tombs** first. This is the 900-page single-volume overview. It gives her the entire timeline so every subsequent book has a skeleton to hang on

**Phase 2 — Period specialists (chronological):**
2. **Morris** (Anglo-Saxons → Norman Conquest) — the deep detail on pre-1066 England
3. **Jones** (Plantagenets → Wars of the Roses) — the medieval dynasty that shaped England
4. **Ackroyd** (Foundation → Tudors → Revolution) — three volumes covering prehistory through 1815
5. **Schama** (Vol 1 → Vol 2 → Vol 3) — the sweep with beautiful prose, different perspective on the same periods

**Phase 3 — The Empire question:**
6. **Ferguson** and **Olusoga** back-to-back — read the triumphalist case first, then the reckoning. The contrast is the point

**Phase 4 — The bookends:**
7. **Wilson** (Victorians) fills in the industrial and cultural detail
8. **Marr** closes the story — the modern Britain that emerged from the wreckage of Empire

*Note: Ackroyd's Foundation overlaps with Morris and Jones; Schama overlaps with everyone. This is intentional — different historians telling the same story illuminate different aspects.*

## Key Themes Across the Books

### Power and Legitimacy
- How kings made themselves legitimate (divine right, conquest, parliamentary consent)
- The recurring pattern: strong monarch → overreach → rebellion → new settlement
- From absolute monarchy to constitutional democracy — not a smooth line but a series of violent corrections

### England vs Britain
- England is not Britain. Tombs writes about the English; Schama writes about Britain
- The union with Scotland (1707), the conquest of Ireland, the absorption of Wales — these are not the same story as English history
- The Empire complicates it further — "British" became a global identity

### Empire and Its Consequences
- Ferguson: the Empire spread trade, rule of law, and parliamentary government. Net positive for the world
- Olusoga: the Empire was built on slavery, extraction, and racial hierarchy. The "benefits" went one way
- Neither is wrong. The section exists so your wife can hold both views simultaneously

### Class and Social Change
- The Norman Conquest created the English class system (French-speaking aristocracy ruling English-speaking peasants)
- The Plantagenets created Parliament (originally to raise taxes, not to represent the people)
- The Victorians created the middle class; the post-war era democratised it further
- Class is the thread that runs through every era of British history
